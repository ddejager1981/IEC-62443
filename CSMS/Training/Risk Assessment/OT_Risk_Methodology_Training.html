Stage 1 · Foundations

Why OT Risk Assessment is
fundamentally different from IT

Before touching any methodology, you must understand why the risk assessment tools you use for enterprise IT will fail — or worse, create new hazards — when applied to Industrial Automation and Control Systems.

⏱ 45 min
📍 IEC 62443-2-1 §4.2
🎯 IT risk professionals

What the standard actually says

IEC 62443-2-1:2010 §4.2.3.1 (SHALL) requires organisations to select a specific risk assessment methodology. But before selecting one, you must understand what makes the IACS environment different — because the methodology must fit the environment, not the other way around.

⚠️
The §4.2.3.1 SHALL requirement: "The organisation SHALL select a particular risk assessment methodology." This is not a suggestion. You must document your chosen methodology and justify the selection. If you choose a methodology that doesn't fit your environment, your risk assessment results will be wrong — and your CSMS will be built on a false foundation.
🖥️ IT security risk assessment
Primary goal: Confidentiality of data
Worst case: Data breach, reputational damage, financial loss
Availability priority: Important but secondary to confidentiality
Downtime tolerance: Hours — acceptable with good BCP
Patch cadence: Monthly patch Tuesday — standard practice
Account lockout: Safe and standard — security best practice
System lifetime: 3–5 years — refresh cycles planned
Vulnerability scanning: Routine — run quarterly or continuously
🏭 OT / IACS risk assessment
Primary goal: Safety, availability, and process integrity
Worst case: Personnel fatality, environmental release, community harm
Availability priority: Highest priority — often more critical than confidentiality
Downtime tolerance: Often zero — unplanned shutdown can itself cause HSE incident
Patch cadence: Vendor-coordinated, outage-window dependent — often annual
Account lockout: Can be catastrophic — locking a HMI operator during an emergency
System lifetime: 20–30+ years — PLCs from the 1990s still in service
Vulnerability scanning: Can crash PLCs — active scanning requires vendor approval

The three risk domains in OT

OT risk assessment must simultaneously address three interlocking domains. No single IT risk methodology handles all three. This is why OT assessment almost always requires a hybrid approach.

🔒 Cyber domain
Attack paths, threat actors, vulnerability exploitation, network exposure, authentication gaps. This is where IT risk tools have the most applicability — but they must be adapted.
⚠️ Physical / Safety domain
What happens physically when a cyber attack succeeds. A manipulated PLC setpoint isn't a data breach — it's a vessel overpressure or a hoist collision. The consequence chain is physical, not digital.
🔄 Operational domain
Production continuity, process integrity, and the risk created by security controls themselves. An access lockout that prevents an operator from responding to an alarm creates a new risk.

Why IT risk methodologies fall short

IT risk practitioners often reach for familiar tools — ISO 27005, FAIR, OCTAVE — when asked to assess OT systems. Here is specifically where and why they fail.

Practical Example Applying ISO 27005 to a refinery DCS — what goes wrong
1
Asset identification
ISO 27005 asks you to identify information assets. The DCS controller is listed as an asset. ✓
Problem: The control program logic, the setpoint table, and the cause-and-effect matrix are not information assets in the IT sense — they are the physical process safety case. Losing their integrity is not a "data integrity" issue — it's a process hazard.
2
Threat identification
ISO 27005 focuses on threats to information confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
Problem: The most dangerous threats to a DCS are not data theft — they are process manipulation (changing a setpoint to 200% of safe operating level), safety system defeat (disconnecting an interlock), and denial of view (flooding the HMI so operators cannot see alarm states). These threat types are not captured in standard IT threat libraries.
3
Impact assessment
ISO 27005 scores impact in terms of financial loss, reputational damage, and regulatory penalty.
Problem: The primary impact of a DCS cyber incident is physical — personnel injury or fatality, environmental release, community evacuation. These are orders of magnitude more significant than financial loss, but ISO 27005 has no native scale for them. Risk scores are systematically underestimated.
4
Control selection
ISO 27005 recommends controls from ISO 27001 Annex A.
Problem: Many standard IT controls create new OT risks. Mandatory password complexity policies on a DCS operator workstation mean operators cannot log in quickly during an emergency. Automated session timeouts on HMIs leave critical control screens unattended. The controls weren't designed with a process safety case in mind.
💡
The IEC 62443-2-1 approach: The standard explicitly recognises this gap. §4.2.3.11 requires that physical, HSE, and cyber security risk results be integrated into a unified risk picture. The methodology you choose must be capable of capturing safety consequences — not just information security impacts.

The IEC 62443 risk model

The standard (§4.2.3) structures risk analysis in a specific sequence. Understanding this sequence is essential before selecting a methodology — your chosen method must be capable of executing each step.

1
High-level risk assessment first §4.2.3.3 SHALL
Characterise threats, general vulnerabilities, and consequences at the system level before going deeper. This produces the risk narrative that management and safety teams need. Annex B is explicit: jumping to detailed vulnerability assessment without this step produces findings that are ignored.
2
System prioritisation §4.2.3.6 SHALL
Rank systems by risk level. Detailed assessment resources go to the highest-priority systems first. The prioritisation criteria (safety consequence × connectivity × existing controls) must be documented and defensible.
3
Detailed vulnerability assessment §4.2.3.7 SHALL
Technical assessment of specific exploitable weaknesses on prioritised systems — firmware versions, authentication gaps, network exposure, unpatched vulnerabilities. This is where ICS-specific tools and expertise are required.
4
Detailed risk assessment using the selected methodology §4.2.3.9 SHALL
Apply your documented methodology to produce scored, prioritised risk statements. Every risk must trace back to a specific threat, a specific vulnerability, and a specific consequence. These results go into the Risk Register (CSMS-RA-008) and drive all countermeasure selection.
🎯
Key insight for IT risk professionals: In IT risk, you often start with vulnerabilities (scan the network, find the CVEs) and work backwards to threat and impact. In OT, you must start with the consequence — what physical harm could occur — and work forwards to identify which threats and vulnerabilities could cause it. The direction of analysis is reversed.

Method 1 · ISA/IEC 62443 Risk Matrix

This is the primary methodology for IACS cyber risk assessment. It is purpose-built for OT environments and produces Target Security Level (SL-T) outputs that directly drive countermeasure selection under the CSMS.

✅ Where it excels
Zone-based model maps directly to OT network architecture. SL-T output ties risk directly to required controls. Handles adversarial cyber threats natively. Aligned to IEC 62443-2-1. Accepted by regulators and insurers. Scalable across full IACS inventories.
⚠️ Where it falls short
Does not deeply analyse the physical consequence chain — you need HAZOP to understand what "manipulation of hoist control" causes physically. SL-T can become subjective without rigorous threat modelling providing input.
Worked Example — Step by Step Assessing a SCADA historian at Sunrise Refinery using IEC 62443 Risk Matrix

Scenario: The SCADA historian at Sunrise Refinery is accessible from the corporate DMZ without authentication. We need to assess the cyber risk using the IEC 62443 risk matrix approach.

1
Define the zone
The historian sits in the Supervisory Zone (Level 3). It connects to the corporate DMZ (external) and to the DCS historian feed (internal, Level 2). This creates a conduit between zones that requires assessment.
2

3/4

Likelihood = 3 (Medium) — Realistic threat with motivated actors and high exposure, but requires some effort to reach the historian.

3

Safety / HSE

Production loss

Consequence = 3 (Critical) — The historian itself is not safety-critical, but it provides a lateral movement path to the DCS. If an attacker pivots from the historian to the DCS, the consequence escalates to catastrophic.

4
Calculate risk level
Likelihood (3) × Consequence (3) = Score 9 HIGH RISK

Score ≥ 9 = HIGH. Countermeasure required within 90 days. The primary countermeasure: require authentication on all historian access, and isolate from the corporate DMZ via a dedicated historian-only interface with strict access controls.

5
Determine Target Security Level (SL-T)
Based on this risk assessment, the historian zone is assigned SL-T 2 — protection against intentional violation using simple means with low resources. The zone must implement countermeasures that achieve SL-T 2: authentication required, firewall-enforced access, audit logging enabled.

Method 2 · HAZOP / LOPA

HAZOP (Hazard and Operability Study) was created for process safety in chemical and oil & gas industries. It is the foundation of physical safety assessment in IACS environments — but it was not designed for cyber threats.

✅ Where it excels in OT
Reveals what cyber-induced deviations cause physically — "what if the pressure signal is 'more' than design?" Integrates with existing process safety documentation. Required for SIL verification under IEC 61511. Familiar to operations and safety engineers who will implement controls.
⚠️ Why it fails as a primary cyber method
Assumes accidental failure — not adversarial intent. Cannot identify cyber attack paths (an attacker who gains access is not a "deviation"). Does not produce SL-T outputs. Meaningless applied to pure software systems like TOS or SCADA historians.
Worked Example HAZOP on RTG hoist control — Sunrise Refinery DCS pressure controller

How HAZOP works: Apply guide words (No, More, Less, Reverse, Other Than) to each parameter in the system to identify deviations and their causes and consequences.

ParameterGuide WordDeviationPossible CauseCyber CauseConsequenceSeverity
Pressure signal MORE Higher pressure reading than actual Sensor failure, impulse line blockage Attacker manipulates sensor reading in DCS Controller reduces feed flow → process starved → shutdown Medium
Pressure signal LESS Lower pressure reading than actual Sensor failure, leak in impulse line Attacker spoofs low pressure reading Controller increases feed → overpressure → potential vessel failure Critical
Setpoint MORE Setpoint raised beyond design Operator error, HMI display fault Attacker modifies setpoint via DCS access Vessel operates above safe limit → PSV lift → loss of containment risk Critical
Interlock signal NO Interlock does not actuate when required SIS failure, logic fault Attacker disables interlock via SIS access Protective shutdown does not occur → major incident potential Catastrophic
💡
What HAZOP tells you that the IEC 62443 matrix doesn't: The physical mechanism by which a cyber attack causes harm. Without HAZOP, you know the historian is a HIGH risk — but you don't know the specific overpressure pathway that makes it consequential. HAZOP provides that physical consequence chain, which then feeds more accurate consequence scores into the IEC 62443 matrix.

Method 3 · STRIDE Threat Modelling

STRIDE (Spoofing · Tampering · Repudiation · Information Disclosure · Denial of Service · Elevation of Privilege) systematically enumerates adversarial threats against software-intensive systems and APIs.

✅ Where it excels in OT
Outstanding for software-intensive systems — TOS, SCADA, historians, AGV navigation, APIs. Specifically designed for adversarial threat identification. Captures V2X communication threats. Handles IT/OT boundary and supply chain threats systematically.
⚠️ Where it falls short
Produces a massive list of threats without inherent prioritisation — you can generate 200+ STRIDE findings for a large system with no sense of which matter. Does not quantify risk. Does not address physical consequence chains.
Worked Example STRIDE applied to an IGV navigation system at a port terminal
STRIDE ThreatApplied to IGV NavigationAttack ExampleOT Consequence

Without risk scoring (from the IEC 62443 matrix), you cannot tell your client whether to fix the Spoofing issue or the DoS issue first. STRIDE provides the input — the IEC 62443 matrix provides the prioritisation.

Method 4 · Bow-Tie Analysis

Bow-Tie maps threats (left side) through a top event to consequences (right side), explicitly showing prevention barriers (left) and recovery/mitigation barriers (right). It bridges the cyber and physical safety worlds in a single visual.

✅ Where it excels in OT
Visually compelling for executives and safety teams. Shows exactly where barriers exist AND where gaps are. Integrates cyber controls with physical safety barriers on a single diagram. The single most effective communication tool for cross-domain cyber-physical risk.
⚠️ Where it falls short
Not a threat identification method — needs HAZOP or STRIDE input to identify the scenarios. Does not scale to full-system enumeration. Resource-intensive to produce properly. Does not produce SL-T outputs alone.

Method 5 · NIST SP 800-30 / 800-82

NIST SP 800-30 is the IT risk assessment guide. SP 800-82 adapts it specifically for ICS environments. For IT professionals moving into OT security, this is often the most familiar framework — but it is not the best fit as a primary OT methodology.

✅ Where it adds value
Best for organisations with existing NIST CSF/RMF programmes extending into OT. Connects IT and OT risk registers in a common language. SP 800-82 Guide to ICS Security provides OT-specific guidance. Useful for SCADA/historian systems at the IT/OT boundary.
⚠️ Why it is not OT-primary
Produces no SL-T output — a gap against IEC 62443-2-1 requirements for CSMS. Weaker on physical and safety consequence analysis. Less accepted by process safety engineers who implement the controls. Requires significant adaptation for real-time OT constraints.
🎯
When to use NIST 800-30/82: Use it as a bridging methodology when your client already has an IT risk programme using NIST and wants to extend it to OT — for example, a manufacturing company with a NIST CSF implementation that needs to bring OT into the scope. Use it alongside IEC 62443 risk matrix, not instead of it. The IEC 62443 matrix provides the SL-T output that NIST cannot.

Recommended combinations by industry

In practice, OT risk assessment always uses a hybrid approach. Here are the proven combinations for major IACS industries, with the justification that would appear in a CSMS-RA-001 methodology selection record.

Port / Logistics Terminal DP World-type automated terminal: RTG, IGV/AGV, ASC, TOS
Primary: IEC 62443 Risk Matrix Supporting: STRIDE (TOS + IGV nav) Supporting: HAZOP (RTG/ASC machinery) Validate: Bow-Tie (key scenarios)

Rationale: The terminal presents three distinct risk environments. The TOS and IGV navigation software require STRIDE to enumerate adversarial attack paths against APIs, V2X communications, and positioning data. The RTG and ASC electromechanical systems require HAZOP to establish the physical consequence chain that a cyber incident would initiate. The IEC 62443 risk matrix integrates both inputs to produce SL-T per zone. Bow-Tie analysis is applied to the three highest-consequence scenarios for executive and HSE communication.

Oil, Gas & Refining Refinery/chemical plant: DCS, SIS, SCADA, PLC
Primary: IEC 62443 Risk Matrix Supporting: HAZOP/LOPA (SIS layer) Validate: Bow-Tie (loss of containment scenarios)

Rationale: Process facilities have mature HAZOP programmes — the cyber risk assessment must integrate with, not duplicate, the existing safety case. HAZOP establishes the physical consequence mechanism for each cyber deviation. LOPA validates that the combination of cyber controls and safety instrumented functions provides sufficient risk reduction. IEC 62443 matrix provides the zone-level SL-T determination. The Triconex SIS requires LOPA specifically because IEC 61511 functional safety requirements mandate it.

Water & Wastewater Treatment and distribution: SCADA, PLC dosing, pumping stations
Primary: IEC 62443 Risk Matrix Supporting: HAZOP (chemical dosing systems) Validate: Bow-Tie (contamination scenarios)

Rationale: Water facilities present a public health risk that elevates the consequence dimension significantly. Chemical dosing system manipulation (chlorine over/under-dosing) is the primary concern — HAZOP is used to establish the specific public health consequence chain. IEC 62443 matrix provides zone SL-T. STRIDE is not primary here as the systems are largely not software-intensive, but should be applied to the SCADA system and any internet-facing remote access points.

🎓 Practitioner level

The seven principles of OT risk assessment

1
Start with physical consequence, not vulnerability
In IT, you scan for vulnerabilities and assess their impact. In OT, you start by asking: what is the worst physical thing that can happen here? Then you work backwards to what cyber events could cause it. The Triconex SIS risk was CRITICAL not because the network exposure was high — but because the consequence was catastrophic. Consequence drives everything.
2
Never apply a single methodology to an OT environment
No single methodology captures all three OT risk domains (cyber, physical, operational). The IEC 62443 risk matrix is your spine — but it needs physical consequence input (HAZOP) for safety-critical systems, and adversarial threat input (STRIDE) for software-intensive systems. Always use at least two methods.
3
The methodology must produce SL-T — everything else is supplementary
Under IEC 62443-2-1, the CSMS requires Target Security Levels to drive countermeasure selection. HAZOP, STRIDE, and Bow-Tie are valuable — but they do not produce SL-T. The IEC 62443 risk matrix must always be part of the approach. Your CSMS-RA-001 must document how SL-T is derived.
4
Security controls must not create safety hazards
Every security control you recommend must be checked for HSE impact. Account lockout: does it lock operators out during emergencies? Encryption: does it add latency that breaks real-time control loops? Patch application: does the reboot create a production hazard? This is the §4.2.3.11 integration requirement — and it is never optional.
5
Follow the Annex B sequence — always
High-level risk assessment before detailed vulnerability assessment. Business rationale before any technical work. The sequence is defined in Annex B for a reason: detailed technical findings presented without a risk narrative are ignored by management. You have seen this happen in IT — it is worse in OT where the audience is operations engineers, not CISOs.
6
Document the methodology selection formally (CSMS-RA-001)
§4.2.3.1 (SHALL) requires a documented methodology selection with justification. This is the document an auditor, regulator, or insurer will ask for first. The decision matrix in Stage 3 of this training generates the justification text. Use it. An undocumented methodology selection is a SHALL non-conformance.
7
The risk register is a living document — risk changes when the system changes
§4.2.3.10 (SHALL) requires documented reassessment triggers. Every time a system changes — new firmware, new network connection, new vendor, new threat intelligence — the risk register must be reviewed. A risk register that is accurate on day one and never updated is not a CSMS — it is a compliance exercise.
